Affordable Housing That Leaves the Poorest Behind

Data shows affordable housing in Manhattan Community Board 1 is built mostly for higher-income households, not the poorest.

By Baron Carr

Nov. 22, 2025

Affordable housing sounds simple, but the numbers in Manhattan Community Board 1 tell a different story. The district has between 600 and 700 total units, but only 86 are affordable for families who earn less than $60,000 a year. These are the residents who face the most pressure. At 30 percent of area median income, a three-person household must earn about $43,000 to qualify for an apartment, according to New York City’s Department of Housing Preservation and Development. This matters now because New York City has a new mayor-elect, Mandami, and families across the city are counting on him to take this crisis seriously.

Who Is CB1’s Affordable Housing Really For?

This chart compares how many affordable units were built at each income level in CB1 with the income a three-person family needs to qualify.

The data shows that the lowest-income families receive the fewest units, while most new apartments labeled affordable require six-figure incomes.

“This is not only about policy. It is about dignity,” said housing advocate Charles Powell. “If families who work every day cannot access homes labeled affordable, then the structure is the problem.”

The gap between required income and available homes continues to push low-income residents out before they can apply.
